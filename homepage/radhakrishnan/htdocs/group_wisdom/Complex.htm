Complex formation with SF1 DBD (aa10-111) and inhibin a promoter SBS

 

General guidelines:

  •  Keep the DNA and protein at room temperature or higher at all times
  • Do not expose the protein to salt at any time
  • Perform the titration by adding the protein to the DNA
  • The rate of addition of protein to DNA is extremely important; once the protein is added to the DNA, a slight haze forms.  This haze should disappear completely before addition of new protein.  If the protein is added too quickly, irreversible precipitate forms.  If formation of the precipitate is minimized as much as possible, the sample will behave better in general.
  • Avoid vortexing the complex solution
  • Perform all concentrating centrifugations at room temperature\
  • Determine the concentration of the complex by measuring the A260 and the calculated extinction coefficient of the double stranded DNA duplex
  • 1:1 Stoichiometry of the protein/DNA complex must be determined by the proton NMR spectrum

 

 

Buffer:

10 mM d-tris acetate

50 mM ZnCl2

2 mM d-DTT

10 % D2O

0.2 % d-glycerol

pH 6.0

 

Protocol:

1)        Dilute protein and DNA with deuterated NMR buffer to 40 mM in separate tubes

2)        Prepare 20% more protein than DNA

3)        Slowly add protein to DNA approximately 1 mL at a time.  Gently swirl until the protein haze goes back into solution.  Keep adding until the haze is slightly persistent and small particles appear to float near the top of the solution. 

4)        Wait approx 30 minutes between each addition.

5)        Keep tube at room temp to help the protein and complex stay in solution

6)        Check the stoichiometry by 1H1D NMR

7)        Concentrate sample to 500 mL at room temperature using Centriprep and Centricon 3000 MWCO at 2000 rpm in IEC centrifuge

8)        Add sample to NMR tube

 

Pitfalls experienced:

  • We have recovered some precipitate in solution by using a sonicating water bath and heating the sample to 42 °C – however this should not be done with the sample in 100% D2O
  • Agitating the sample by vortexing or shaking in the incubator seems to increase the amount of precipitate formed.
  • The complex cannot be successfully resuspended after lyophilization.
  • We have noticed the precipitate is not just protein alone, but complex as well (as indicated by the decrease in DNA concentration).
  • We tend to lose 50% of our starting material in making the final NMR sample (eg. 2 mM starting material becomes ~1 mM NMR sample).
  • It is typical to see a large amount of precipitate in the centrifuge concentrator.
